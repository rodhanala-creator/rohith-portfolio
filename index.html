Case 01 Healthcare · Operations

The dental practice that was drowning in its own admin.

A five-chair dental practice in the South of England was busy in all the wrong ways. Patients called, emailed, walked in, and messaged on social. Each channel had its own rhythm — and its own pile of sticky notes. By the time a receptionist confirmed an emergency slot, two more enquiries had slipped past unanswered. The team was working harder every week, but the day still ended with a queue of half-finished tasks.

I spent the first two days shadowing the front desk and mapping every touchpoint a patient went through, from the first "hello" to the post-treatment follow-up. The bottleneck wasn't effort. It was that enquiries had no single home, no triage rules, and no automatic safety net. So I built one. Every channel now feeds a shared intake. Urgent cases auto-route to an emergency queue. Routine requests get a templated reply and a calendar link within seconds. Reminders go out without a human touching them.

Six weeks in, the practice had cut average first-response from six hours to under two minutes, recovered roughly seven hours of admin time per week, and — for the first time in years — closed each day with an empty inbox. The team didn't get more tools. They got fewer decisions to make.

Stack Make.com · Tally · Calendly · Gmail API · Airtable · Twilio · OpenAI (intent classification & reply drafting)

Case 02 Service business · Lead generation

A plumbing business with plenty of leads — and nowhere to put them.

The owner of a regional plumbing business showed me his "system" on a Tuesday morning: three phone screenshots, a notebook, and a Gmail inbox with 1,400 unread emails. Leads were arriving from Google Ads, the website, Facebook, and word of mouth — but most of them never heard back within the first hour, and that's the window where service-business customers actually book. He wasn't losing work to competitors. He was losing it to silence.

I rebuilt the front of the funnel as a single, calm pipeline. A new landing page, one shared form, and an auto-acknowledgement that goes out within seconds — drafted by AI from the enquiry text so it reads like a human, not a template. Every lead lands in a four-stage board: New, Contacted, Qualified, Booked. Drip sequences nudge warm leads. A re-engagement flow wakes up dormant ones after thirty days. The owner finally had one place to look.

Within a month, first-response time dropped from hours to under three minutes, conversion on website leads moved from 19% to 31%, and eighteen previously-cold contacts re-engaged on their own. The team stopped being reactive. The system did the chasing.

Stack Framer · Tally · Airtable · Make.com · Resend · OpenAI (reply drafting & lead qualification) · Slack notifications

Case 03 Web app · Bookings & client ops

BookFlow — the booking + client app I built when no SaaS quite fit.

After two automation projects in the same month, I noticed the same pattern: small service businesses kept stitching together a calendar, a spreadsheet, a CRM, and a messaging tool — and none of it talked to itself. They didn't need another all-in-one platform with sixty features they'd never use. They needed something simple, fast, and theirs. So I designed and built BookFlow as a working prototype.

BookFlow is a small, opinionated web app: today's schedule on the left, the live booking pipeline in the middle, a client directory you can search instantly, and an AI panel that drafts the next message based on the booking's context — confirmation, reminder, follow-up, or a polite "running ten minutes late." Adding a booking takes three keystrokes. Marking it done updates the client's history automatically. The whole interface fits on one screen and runs in the browser.

I built it with vanilla JavaScript, CSS Grid, and a thin local data layer — no framework, no build step, no backend lock-in. The point wasn't to ship a SaaS. It was to prove that a focused tool, designed around one workflow, beats a bloated platform every time. The demo below is the real thing — every interaction is live.
